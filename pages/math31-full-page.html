What is Math 31?

Math 31 is a calculus course that serves as an introduction to differential and integral calculus. It is typically taken after completing Math 20 and is often a prerequisite for more advanced mathematics courses.

Second Derivative Tips

The second derivative measures the instantaneous curvature at a point, indicating whether and how the slope changes before and after that point.


Key Concepts

  • Second derivative equals zero: When \( f''(x) = 0 \) at a point, this indicates that the first derivative \( f'(x) \) is flat (has a horizontal tangent) at that point.
  • Flat first derivative: If \( f'(x) \) is flat at a point, it means the original function \( f(x) \) has no change in slope at that point, indicating no concavity at that specific location.
  • Concavity around the point: As long as the first derivative is not linear, there will be concavity both before and after this point on the original function \( f(x) \).
  • Types of points: At such a point on \( f(x) \), you either have:
    • A very flat vertex (like in \( x^4 \)), or
    • A point of inflection

The Second Derivative Test

If the first derivative reveals a critical point (where \( f'(x) = 0 \)), the second derivative can determine the concavity at that vertex (how the slope changes before and after):

  • If \( f''(x) > 0 \): The point is a minimum. This is because the change in slope is positive (the "velocity" of the slope is positive). Any "bowl" shape indicates that the slope is consistently increasing.
  • If \( f''(x) < 0 \): The point is a maximum. The change in slope is negative, meaning the slope is consistently decreasing (an inverted bowl shape).

Exception to the Second Derivative Test

Important: If both the slope (\( f'(x) \)) and the concavity (\( f''(x) \)) are zero at a point, then there is no change in slope at that location on the original function. In this case, the second derivative test fails, and you must manually apply the first derivative test to determine the nature of the critical point.


Summary

In essence, any "bowl" shaped curve (whether opening upward or downward) indicates that the slope is either consistently increasing or consistently decreasing. The second derivative test leverages this property to classify critical points efficiently, except in cases where both derivatives vanish simultaneously.
